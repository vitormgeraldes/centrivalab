Orthogonal Centrifugal Membrane Filter (O-CMF)

CentrivaLab™ is the commercial platform built on the Orthogonal Centrifugal Membrane Filter (O-CMF) principle. In scientific communication, the correct technical term remains O-CMF.

O-CMF uses centrifugal hydrostatics to create transmembrane pressure inside rotor-matched cartridge geometries. This provides a compact route to membrane screening workflows within standard centrifuge infrastructure.

Validated against crossflow NF screening

Published validation work compared O-CMF against a reference NF crossflow bench-scale unit (SEPA CF Cell) using two commercial nanofiltration membranes and standard reference solutes including NaCl, MgSO₄, glucose, sucrose and lactose.

The validation criteria covered membrane representativeness, water permeability, permeation fluxes, rejection values, osmotic-pressure robustness and concentration-polarization behaviour.

Uniform-pressure design

The defining geometric feature of the O-CMF is the orthogonal placement of a flat membrane coupon relative to the centrifugal acceleration. This was designed to address non-uniform transmembrane pressure and excessive local polarization commonly associated with conventional centrifugal membrane filters.

The compact cartridge architecture also simplifies membrane replacement and creates a geometry that is easier to analyse, benchmark and standardise experimentally.

Concentration polarization and CFD insight

CFD and experimental analysis showed that the concentrate chamber is homogenized by centrifugal convection and that time-averaged concentration polarization is nearly uniform across most of the membrane surface.

Under demanding conditions, the concentration-polarization index remained below 2.0, while in industrially relevant NF flux windows around 20–30 L·h⁻¹·m⁻² it commonly falls into a much lower range, often around 0.2–0.4.

Flux regime Typical interpretation
20–30 L·h⁻¹·m⁻² Typical NF/RO operating window with low concentration polarization
~60 L·h⁻¹·m⁻² Crossflow-relevant range where polarization becomes more evident
Up to ~160 L·h⁻¹·m⁻² Extreme screening regime; polarization remains controlled rather than dominant

Rotor-matched cartridge families

The commercial platform is being focused around two rotor-native product families.

CentrivaLab 50/34

Designed for 50 mL conical tubes and fixed-angle rotors around 34°, especially JA-20 class 8 × 50 mL rotors. This is the current reference family for higher-pressure NF/RO screening.

CentrivaLab 94/25

Planned for 94 mL rotor-class geometries around 25–26°, with emphasis on Sigma 4 × 94 mL and 6 × 94 mL fixed-angle rotors. This family is strategically important because it supports compact bench-top centrifuge workflows at approximately 15,000–20,000 rpm.

Materials strategy

Public technology communication should not expose unnecessary manufacturing detail. At product level, CentrivaLab cartridges can be implemented in prototype-grade acrylic resins and in higher-performance engineering polymers such as PEEK, PAEK and PPS, depending on the workflow, chemistry and manufacturing maturity.

Final wetted-material stacks should be controlled through specification pages, datasheets and compatibility notes, not improvised in broad marketing text.

Scope

The published validation work is centered on aqueous nanofiltration screening, with direct comparison against a reference crossflow unit. The broader pressure range and design logic of O-CMF also support future work in UF and RO-oriented workflows.
